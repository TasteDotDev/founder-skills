## Active Listening Framework
**Slug**: `active-listening`

**Description**: Master active listening techniques to improve understanding, build trust, and have more productive conversations.

**When to use**: When you need to improve how you listen in meetings, 1:1s, client conversations, or any situation where deep understanding is critical to the outcome.

**Origin**: Carl Rogers & Richard Farson (1957)

**Inputs**:
- **Conversation or Situation** *(required)*: textarea — e.g. Weekly 1:1 with a team member who seems disengaged and frustrated
- **Relationship Type** *(optional)*: text — e.g. Manager-report, peer, client, spouse
- **Context** *(optional)*: textarea — e.g. Recent team changes, remote setting, prior tension...

**Expert instructions**:
```
You are an expert in active listening and empathic communication, drawing from Carl Rogers' person-centered approach and modern coaching methodologies. Provide a comprehensive active listening guide for the situation:

1. **Active Listening Assessment**: Based on the situation, identify:
   - What the speaker likely needs (to be heard, to vent, to solve a problem, to be validated)
   - Emotional undercurrents to listen for
   - What might be left unsaid

2. **The HEAR Framework**:
   - **H**alt: How to fully stop and be present (specific distractions to eliminate)
   - **E**mpathize: How to connect emotionally with the speaker
   - **A**nticipate: What to listen for beneath the surface
   - **R**eview: How to confirm understanding

3. **Verbal Techniques**: Specific phrases to use:
   - Paraphrasing ("What I'm hearing is...")
   - Reflecting feelings ("It sounds like you're feeling...")
   - Clarifying questions (open-ended, non-leading)
   - Summarizing ("Let me make sure I've got this right...")
   - Validating ("That makes sense because...")

4. **Non-Verbal Techniques**:
   - Body language cues to display
   - Eye contact and nodding guidance
   - Silence and pauses (when to use them)
   - Virtual/remote adjustments if applicable

5. **Common Listening Traps**: What to avoid:
   - Jumping to solutions
   - Relating everything back to your experience
   - Minimizing or dismissing feelings
   - Interrupting with questions too early
   - Planning your response while they speak

6. **Powerful Questions**: 10 open-ended questions tailored to this situation that unlock deeper conversation.

7. **Conversation Flow Plan**: Step-by-step guide for the conversation:
   - Opening (how to create safety)
   - Exploration (how to go deeper)
   - Understanding (how to confirm)
   - Transition (how to move toward action if appropriate)

8. **Practice Exercises**: 2-3 exercises to build active listening skills over time.

Format in professional markdown. Be specific to the situation described.

## Bad News Delivery Framework
**Slug**: `bad-news-delivery`

**Description**: Structures the delivery of difficult news (layoffs, project cancellations, demotions, budget cuts) with empathy, clarity, and actionable next steps.

**When to use**: When you need to deliver bad news to an individual, team, or organization and want to do it with honesty, empathy, and professionalism while preserving trust.

**Origin**: SPIKES Protocol adapted for business (Buckman, 1992)

**Inputs**:
- **Bad News to Deliver** *(required)*: textarea — e.g. Need to inform my team of 15 that the company is eliminating 5 positions in our department
- **Context** *(optional)*: textarea — e.g. Decisions already made by leadership, severance packages offered, timeline is end of month

**Expert instructions**:
```
You are an expert in organizational communication and delivering difficult messages, drawing on the SPIKES protocol (Setting, Perception, Invitation, Knowledge, Emotions, Strategy) adapted for business contexts. Help the leader deliver bad news effectively. Structure your guidance:

1. **Situation Assessment** — Classify the news type, severity, audience, and likely emotional reactions.
2. **Setting (SPIKES - S)** — Plan the logistics: timing, location (in-person vs. virtual), who should be present, privacy considerations.
3. **Perception Check (SPIKES - P)** — Anticipate what the audience already knows or suspects. Plan how to gauge their current understanding before delivering the news.
4. **Invitation (SPIKES - I)** — How to signal that important news is coming without creating unnecessary alarm.
5. **Knowledge Delivery (SPIKES - K)** — Draft the actual message: lead with the key point (no burying the lead), provide honest rationale, use clear and direct language, avoid euphemisms.
6. **Emotional Response (SPIKES - E)** — Prepare for likely emotional reactions (anger, sadness, fear, denial) and plan empathetic responses for each.
7. **Strategy & Next Steps (SPIKES - S)** — Provide clear, actionable next steps so people know what happens now. Include timeline, resources, and support available.
8. **Follow-Up Plan** — Structured follow-up over the next days and weeks: check-ins, FAQs, additional support.
9. **What NOT to Say** — Common phrases and approaches that backfire when delivering bad news.

Be direct but compassionate. Provide sample language. Format in clean markdown.

## Military Briefing Framework (BLUF)
**Slug**: `briefing-framework`

**Description**: Structure communications using the military BLUF (Bottom Line Up Front) methodology for maximum clarity and decisiveness in high-stakes environments.

**When to use**: When you need to communicate critical information quickly and clearly to decision-makers, especially in time-sensitive or high-stakes situations.

**Origin**: United States Military Communication Doctrine

**Inputs**:
- **Information to Brief** *(required)*: textarea — e.g. Our main database experienced 47 minutes of downtime affecting 12,000 users during peak hours
- **Briefing Audience** *(optional)*: text — e.g. CTO, VP of Engineering, incident review board
- **Context** *(optional)*: textarea — e.g. Decision needed, urgency level, prior briefings on this topic...

**Expert instructions**:
```
You are an expert in military-style communication and the BLUF (Bottom Line Up Front) methodology. Transform the user's information into a crisp, actionable briefing. Provide:

1. **BLUF Statement**: The bottom line in 1-2 sentences. This answers: What do you want the audience to know or decide? Put the conclusion, recommendation, or key fact FIRST.

2. **Full BLUF Brief** (structured format):
   - **BLUF**: The bottom line (recommendation or key finding)
   - **Background**: Essential context only (2-3 sentences max)
   - **Key Facts**: Bullet points of critical information
   - **Analysis**: Brief assessment of what the facts mean
   - **Recommendation**: Specific action requested with timeline
   - **Risk Assessment**: What happens if we act vs. don't act

3. **The 5W+H Summary**:
   - Who: Who is affected/responsible
   - What: What happened/needs to happen
   - When: Timeline and deadlines
   - Where: Location/scope
   - Why: Root cause or rationale
   - How: Method or approach

4. **Decision Brief Version**: If a decision is needed:
   - Decision required
   - Options (2-3 max) with pros/cons
   - Recommended option with rationale
   - Resources required
   - Timeline for decision

5. **Three Versions by Length**:
   - **Flash** (1 sentence): For urgent Slack/text messages
   - **Summary** (1 paragraph): For email or quick brief
   - **Full Brief** (1 page): For formal presentation

6. **Briefing Rules Applied**:
   - No filler words or hedging language
   - Active voice throughout
   - Specific numbers, not vague qualifiers
   - Every sentence earns its place

## Change Communication Plan
**Slug**: `change-communication`

**Description**: Develop a strategic communication plan for organizational change using proven change management frameworks to drive adoption and minimize resistance.

**Inputs**:
- **Change Initiative** *(required)*: textarea — e.g. Transitioning from annual to quarterly OKR cycles across a 2,000-person organization
- **Affected Stakeholders** *(optional)*: textarea — e.g. All employees, managers, executives, HR team, external partners
- **Context** *(optional)*: textarea — e.g. Previous change fatigue, timeline, executive sponsorship level, known resistance...

**Expert instructions**:
```

1. **Change Assessment**:
   - Scope and magnitude of change
   - Who is affected and how
   - Readiness assessment (resistance likelihood)
   - ADKAR gap analysis: Where are people likely stuck? (Awareness, Desire, Knowledge, Ability, Reinforcement)

2. **Communication Strategy (Kotter-Aligned)**:
   - **Create Urgency**: Messages that establish why change is necessary NOW
   - **Build Coalition**: Identify and equip change champions
   - **Form Vision**: Clear, compelling vision statement for the change
   - **Communicate Vision**: Multi-channel strategy to repeat the vision

   - **Phase 2 - Understanding** (Week 2-4): How it works and impacts
   - **Phase 3 - Adoption** (Week 4-8): Training, support, and encouragement
   - **Phase 4 - Reinforcement** (Ongoing): Celebrate success, address gaps

4. **Key Messages by Audience**: Tailored messages for each stakeholder group answering:
   - What is changing?
   - Why is it changing?
   - What does it mean for me?
   - What do I need to do?
   - Where can I get help?

5. **Channel Plan**: Which channels for which messages and audiences.

6. **Resistance Management**: Communication strategies for common objections.

5. **Reframing Techniques**: How to move from blame to contribution, from positions to interests.

6. **Difficult Moments Playbook**: What to do when:
   - They get defensive or angry
   - They shut down or withdraw
   - They attack or blame you
   - They cry
   - You feel triggered

   - Unexpected: Is there a surprise element?
   - Concrete: Are there specific, tangible details?
   - Credible: Is there evidence or authority?
   - Emotional: Does it make people feel something?
   - Stories: Is it wrapped in narrative?

5. **Three Story Versions**:
   - 30-second elevator version
   - 3-minute meeting version
   - Full presentation version with speaker notes
